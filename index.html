Research Focus

Three threads that keep showing up in my notebooks, tied together by one question: can a system know how much to trust itself?

Calibration & uncertainty

Studying why deep networks grow overconfident as they train, and how to correct it — from post-hoc temperature scaling to architectures that separate what a model knows from how sure it is.

Memory architectures

Designing longitudinal memory for AI agents: atoms, bonds, and orbital shells that let a system retrieve the right context without drowning in everything it's ever seen.

Multi-agent systems

Currently defining a new direction on verifier drift — whether a judge model watching a self-improving loop quietly collapses toward rubber-stamping "yes," and how early that's detectable.

Calibration

Geometric Feature Orthogonal Decomposition for CNN calibration — a training framework that splits feature space into a discriminative subspace and an uncertainty subspace.

  • Gram-Schmidt initialization plus a Frobenius orthogonality penalty to keep the two subspaces genuinely independent
  • Combined with JSR, AHTS, CCKD, and TTSE for calibration and accuracy gains together, not traded off

Layer-wise Calibration in CNNs

Deep Learning

Linear-probed all 54 residual blocks of a ResNet-110 on CIFAR-100 to trace how miscalibration develops layer by layer, finding a consistent three-phase lifecycle.

  • Early layers: ECE 0.01–0.05 — well calibrated by default
  • Middle layers: ECE peaks near 0.38, MCE near 0.85 — confidence runs far ahead of accuracy
  • Late layers: a "confidence correction phase" pulls ECE back down to ~0.056
